Every sales team has the same problem: your best rep handles objections perfectly, and everyone else stumbles. The knowledge is locked in individual heads. Claude's CoWork product lets you build a shared objection library that captures how your top performers handle pushback — and makes that available to everyone.

This isn't a static document. It's a living system that improves with every deal.

## Why CoWork for Objection Handling

CoWork is Claude's collaborative product — think of it as a persistent workspace where your team contributes knowledge and Claude helps organize, retrieve, and apply it. For objection handling, this means:

- **One source of truth** for how to handle any objection
- **Real examples** from actual deals, not theoretical responses
- **Pattern recognition** across your whole team's experience
- **Instant retrieval** during live calls when reps need help fast

### Step 2: Build Response Templates

For each objection, you want three things: the acknowledgment, the reframe, and the proof point.

<PromptBlock
  title="Generate objection responses"
  prompt="For each of these objections, generate a complete response using the Acknowledge-Reframe-Prove framework.\n\nOBJECTIONS:\n[List your objections here — paste the output from the previous prompt or your own list]\n\nFor each objection, provide:\n\n1. ACKNOWLEDGE (1 sentence): Validate their concern without agreeing with it. Show you've heard this before.\n2. REFRAME (2-3 sentences): Shift the conversation from their concern to the real issue or opportunity they're missing.\n3. PROVE (1-2 sentences): A specific data point, case study reference, or question that makes the reframe credible.\n4. QUESTION: A follow-up question that advances the conversation rather than letting the objection stall it.\n\nWrite these in conversational tone — how a confident rep would actually say this on a call, not how it would read in a training manual. No corporate speak.\n\nAlso tag each response with:\n- Difficulty: Easy / Medium / Hard\n- When you hear it: Early / Mid / Late in the sales cycle\n- Who says it: Champion / Economic Buyer / Technical Evaluator / End User"
/>

<TipCallout type="tip">
The best objection responses come from your actual deals. After each prompt-generated response, add a real example from your team. "Here's how Mike handled this with Acme Corp last month..." Real stories are 10x more useful than generic scripts.
</TipCallout>

## Quick-Reference Templates by Category

### Price Objections

<ScenarioCard
  scenario="The prospect says: 'This is way more expensive than what we're paying now.'"
  answer="Acknowledge the price difference, then shift to total cost. Ask what they're spending in time, errors, and missed opportunities with their current approach. Most 'cheaper' solutions cost more when you factor in everything. Get specific: 'How many hours per week does your team spend on [the thing your tool automates]?' Then do the math together."
  prompt="A prospect told me '[your product] costs 3x what we pay for [competitor]. Help me build a total cost of ownership comparison that includes: their team's time on manual work the competitor doesn't automate, revenue lost from the capabilities they don't have, and the cost of the workarounds they've built. Make it specific to their situation: [describe their situation]."
/>

### Timing Objections

<ScenarioCard
  scenario="The prospect says: 'This isn't the right time. Let's revisit in Q3.'"
  answer="Timing objections are usually about priority, not timing. The question to ask: 'What would need to change between now and Q3 for this to move up the priority list?' If they can answer that, you have a path forward. If they can't, the real objection is something else — usually need or authority."
  prompt="A prospect wants to push to Q3. They said: '[their exact words]'. Help me: (1) identify what's really behind the timing objection, (2) craft a response that respects their timeline while creating urgency around the cost of waiting, and (3) propose a low-commitment next step that keeps the deal alive without pressure."
/>

### Authority Objections

<ScenarioCard
  scenario="The prospect says: 'I need to run this by my boss before we can move forward.'"
  answer="Don't fight it — enable it. Your prospect just told you they're your champion but they need help selling internally. Your job shifts from selling to them to arming them to sell for you. Ask: 'What will your boss want to know?' Then build them a one-page internal pitch they can forward."
  prompt="My champion at [company] needs to get approval from [boss title]. Help me create a one-page internal justification document they can forward. It should include: the business problem in one sentence, the proposed solution in one sentence, expected ROI with specific numbers from our conversation, implementation timeline, and a comparison to the status quo (doing nothing). Format it so it reads like an internal memo, not a vendor pitch."
/>

<TipCallout type="note">
The best objection libraries aren't the biggest — they're the most specific. Five real responses from actual deals that worked are worth more than fifty generic scripts. Prioritize adding real examples from your team over generating more theoretical responses.
</TipCallout>
