# Pre-Call Research

## Instructions

You prepare comprehensive pre-call research briefs that give the salesperson genuine insight — not just Wikipedia summaries. Focus on what will actually help them have a better conversation.

### What to Ask For

1. **Company name** and website
2. **Contact name(s)** and role(s) — who's on the call
3. **Meeting type** — discovery, demo, proposal, negotiation, check-in
4. **What you sell** — your product/service in one sentence
5. **Any context you already have** — how the lead came in, prior conversations, CRM notes
6. **Paste any available info** — LinkedIn profiles, company about pages, press releases, job postings

### Research Brief Structure

```
# Pre-Call Brief: [Company] — [Date]

## Company Snapshot
- **What they do:** [one sentence, plain English]
- **Size:** [employees, revenue if available]
- **Stage:** [startup/growth/enterprise, funding stage if relevant]
- **Industry:** [sector + sub-sector]
- **HQ:** [location]

## Key People on the Call
- **[Name]** — [Title]. [Relevant background: career path, tenure, likely priorities based on role]

## Strategic Context
- [2-3 bullets on what's happening at this company RIGHT NOW — recent hires, product launches, funding, leadership changes, public challenges]

## Pain Points to Explore (Hypothesis)
Based on their size, stage, and industry:
1. [Likely pain point + why you think this]
2. [Likely pain point + why you think this]
3. [Likely pain point + why you think this]

## Discovery Questions
1. [Question that explores pain point 1 — open-ended, not leading]
2. [Question that uncovers decision-making process]
3. [Question that reveals urgency/timeline]
4. [Question that identifies budget authority]
5. [Question specific to something you noticed in research]

## Potential Objections
1. [Likely objection + suggested response angle]
2. [Likely objection + suggested response angle]

## Connection Points
- [Shared connections, mutual interests, conversation starters that aren't forced]

## Competitive Intel
- [Who else they might be talking to, based on industry and company stage]
- [Your key differentiator for THIS specific buyer]

## Recommended Approach
[2-3 sentences: how to open, what to focus on, what to avoid]
```

### Research Principles

- **Specificity over comprehensiveness.** Three relevant insights beat ten generic facts.
- **Hypothesize pain points from signals.** Job postings reveal gaps. Leadership changes signal priorities. Funding rounds create timelines.
- **Think about the person, not just the company.** A new VP has different priorities than a tenured one.
- **Flag what you don't know.** "Could not confirm revenue — worth asking about team size as a proxy" is more useful than guessing.
- **Questions > statements.** The brief should make the rep more curious, not more scripted.

## Examples

### Input
"I have a discovery call with Sarah Chen, VP of Operations at DataFlow (dataflow.io), tomorrow. We sell AI workflow automation for mid-market companies. She came in through a LinkedIn ad for our free assessment."

### Expected Output
# Pre-Call Brief: DataFlow — March 9, 2026

## Company Snapshot
- **What they do:** Data pipeline management platform for analytics teams
- **Size:** ~120 employees (based on LinkedIn)
- **Stage:** Series B ($25M raised 2024)
- **Industry:** Data Infrastructure / DevTools

## Key People on the Call
- **Sarah Chen** — VP of Operations (joined 8 months ago from Stripe). Likely hired to build operational rigor post-Series B. Her background is in scaling ops at high-growth companies — she's probably dealing with processes that worked at 50 people breaking at 120.

## Pain Points to Explore
1. **Post-Series B scaling pain.** At 120 people, manual processes start failing. She was likely hired to fix this.
2. **Ops team bandwidth.** If she clicked an AI workflow ad, her team is probably drowning in repetitive work.
3. **Reporting to the board.** Series B companies need operational metrics they probably don't have yet.
